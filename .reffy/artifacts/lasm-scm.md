# Logical Assemblies

## Structural Consequence in the Age of Agentic Systems

### Abstract

As artificial intelligence transitions from passive prediction systems to active agents capable of planning, reasoning, and executing work, organizations face a new challenge: preserving meaning. Data can be copied. Models can be replaced. Applications can be rebuilt. Yet the underlying structure through which an organization understands itself—its concepts, relationships, constraints, events, and operational truths—remains difficult to formalize and even harder to maintain.

This paper proposes that **Logical Assemblies** represent an emerging class of organizational asset: machine-readable domain knowledge capable of preserving semantic coherence across changing technologies. Viewed through the lens of the Structural Consequence Model, Logical Assemblies can be understood as mechanisms for increasing the ratio of organizational signal to entropy. Their importance lies not merely in enabling better AI systems, but in creating durable structures through which consequence can accumulate over time.

---

## The Problem of Organizational Entropy

Every organization operates under constant entropy.

Employees leave. Systems are replaced. Vendors change. Processes evolve. Terminology drifts. New technologies emerge. Every change introduces friction between what the organization actually knows and what its systems are capable of representing.

Historically, organizations have attempted to manage this entropy through increasingly sophisticated abstractions:

* Databases captured facts.
* ERDs captured structure.
* Canonical data models captured consistency.
* Enterprise architectures captured systems.
* Knowledge graphs captured relationships.
* Ontologies captured meaning.

Each represented an attempt to preserve organizational signal against the natural tendency toward fragmentation.

Yet most of these approaches ultimately remained disconnected from operational reality. They became documentation rather than infrastructure.

The arrival of agentic AI changes this equation.

For the first time, machine systems require not only access to information but access to meaning.

An autonomous system cannot reliably act within a domain it does not understand.

---

## Structural Consequence as a Framework

The Structural Consequence Model proposes a simple relationship:

**Consequence = Signal / Entropy**

with consequence accumulating through time.

In this framework:

* Signal represents retained structure.
* Entropy represents decay, churn, and loss of coherence.
* Time integrates the effects of both.

Applied to organizations, signal consists of the durable understanding of how a business operates:

* What entities exist.
* How they relate.
* What events matter.
* What constraints govern behavior.
* What outcomes are considered successful.

Entropy consists of everything that erodes that understanding:

* Siloed systems.
* Inconsistent terminology.
* Tribal knowledge.
* Organizational turnover.
* Vendor-specific abstractions.
* Unstructured documentation.

Viewed this way, enterprise architecture itself becomes a battle between signal preservation and semantic decay.

The question becomes:

**What structures maximize retained organizational signal while minimizing semantic entropy?**

Logical Assemblies may represent one answer.

## What Is a Logical Assembly?

A Logical Assembly is a machine-readable representation of domain reality.

Unlike a database schema, it is not primarily concerned with storage.

Unlike an API specification, it is not primarily concerned with interfaces.

Unlike a knowledge graph, it is not primarily concerned with relationships.

Instead, a Logical Assembly combines:

* Concepts
* Relationships
* Constraints
* Events
* Policies
* Process semantics
* Evaluation criteria

into a coherent representation of a domain.

If source code describes software behavior, a Logical Assembly describes domain behavior.

For example, a dealership's CRM may contain customer records.

A Logical Assembly contains the concept of Customer itself:

* How customers are created.
* How ownership changes.
* How customers relate to vehicles.
* How trust accumulates.
* What events alter customer state.
* What constitutes successful service.

Applications become temporary projections.

The Assembly becomes the durable artifact.

## The Historical Pattern

Logical Assemblies do not emerge from nowhere.

They sit at the convergence of several historical attempts to bridge engineering systems and business reality.

### Expert Systems

The expert systems movement attempted to encode human expertise into executable rules.

The insight was correct:

Knowledge could be operationalized.

The limitation was that rules alone could not capture the full complexity of organizational reality.

### Canonical Data Models

Enterprise architects attempted to create unified representations of business entities.

These models reduced integration costs but generally remained tied to data exchange rather than semantic understanding.

### The Semantic Web

The Semantic Web introduced ontologies, formal relationships, and machine-readable meaning.

Its ambition was enormous:

Create a shared representation of reality understandable by both humans and machines.

Its challenge was adoption.

The infrastructure often exceeded the practical demand.

### Digital Twins

Digital twins reintroduced operational relevance by linking models directly to real-world systems.

For the first time, representations became synchronized with reality rather than merely describing it.

Logical Assemblies inherit lessons from all of these traditions.

They combine semantic representation with operational utility.

## Why Agentic AI Changes Everything

Previous generations of software required data.

Agentic systems require world models.

A workflow application only needs to know what fields exist.

An autonomous agent must understand:

* What those fields mean.
* Which actions are valid.
* Which outcomes are desirable.
* Which constraints cannot be violated.

This creates a new economic reality.

The value shifts away from models themselves and toward the structures that govern their behavior.

A foundation model may change every six months.

A Logical Assembly may persist for decades.

This inversion mirrors a previous transition in software.

Organizations once believed applications were assets.

Eventually they realized source code was the asset.

Applications became deployments.

Similarly:

Data may become telemetry.

Applications may become projections.

The business itself may become the deployment.

When a Logical Assembly changes, the organization is not merely updating documentation, schemas, or software. It is changing the structure from which roles, workflows, policies, evaluations, automations, and customer experiences can be instantiated.

Logical Assemblies may become the source code of organizational meaning.

## Logical Assemblies and Private Evals

A second development reinforces this trend.

As Satya Nadella recently observed:

"Private evals are the new IP."

The statement becomes more profound when viewed through the lens of Logical Assemblies.

An evaluation is not merely a benchmark.

An evaluation encodes:

* Domain assumptions.
* Success criteria.
* Operational constraints.
* Expected reasoning paths.

In effect, an evaluation is a test of whether an agent correctly understands a domain.

This creates a feedback loop:

Logical Assembly → Evaluation → Agent Behavior

The Assembly defines reality.

The evaluations define successful operation within that reality.

The agents are selected and aligned based on their ability to satisfy those evaluations.

Together they form a system for preserving organizational signal.

## Assemblies as Structural Capital

The most important implication may be economic rather than technical.

Organizations have historically accumulated several forms of capital:

* Financial capital
* Physical capital
* Human capital
* Intellectual capital

Logical Assemblies suggest the emergence of a new category:

**Structural Capital**

Structural Capital is machine-readable organizational understanding.

It consists not merely of information but of the relationships and constraints that give information meaning.

Two companies may possess identical datasets.

Two companies may even use identical AI models.

Yet if one organization possesses a richer, more coherent Logical Assembly, it can produce dramatically different outcomes.

Because consequence emerges not from information alone but from structured information.

In Structural Consequence terms:

The assembly increases retained signal.

The resulting organization accumulates consequence faster than competitors operating under greater semantic entropy.

---

## Conclusion

The history of enterprise technology can be viewed as a continuous effort to preserve signal against entropy.

Databases preserved facts.

Models preserved structure.

Ontologies preserved meaning.

Digital twins preserved synchronization.

Logical Assemblies may represent the next step in this progression.

Their significance is not that they enable smarter agents.

Their significance is that they create durable, machine-readable representations of organizational reality.

Viewed through the Structural Consequence Model, Logical Assemblies become more than technical artifacts.

They become mechanisms for consequence accumulation.

As AI systems become increasingly interchangeable, the most valuable organizational asset may no longer be data, software, or models.

It may be the structure through which an organization understands itself.

The organizations that successfully encode that understanding into durable Logical Assemblies may discover that they are not merely building better AI systems.

They are building persistent consequence.
